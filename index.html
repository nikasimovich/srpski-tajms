Forgotten Letters

Technology governs how we communicate. Many of the defaults we’re familiar with are rooted in English because of the internet’s early origins. Communicating in English became standard, and keyboards and language support echoed this merit. In the former Yugoslavia where both a Serbian Cyrillic script and a Latin alphabet are used simultaneously, this mirrored cultural changes happening during the dissolution of the country in the 1990s.

“Cyrillic is our cultural good,” says Olivera Stojadinović, a type designer and professor at The University of the Arts in Belgrade who is committed to preserving the alphabet’s usage online. This project is a study on the Serbian Cyrillic alphabet and its digital history.

The Serbian Alphabet

Serbia has two official alphabets: the ćirilica, or Serbian Cyrillic, and the Serbian Latin script. While similar to Russian, there are seven distinct characters that highlight the phonetic quality of the language. When Cyrillic support is offered, typically the focus is on the Russian alphabet.

YUSCII

YUSCII was the Yugoslavian character adaption of ASCII (American Standard Code for Information Interchange). Essentially, it was a hacked solution that replaced less common characters with Yugoslavian ones. This was the only way of typing with a Serbian Cyrillic typeface until 1997.

Because typing correctly required extra effort, many internet users opted to use the closest English letters in place of the Serbian ones, altering the pronounciation and meaning of words. This practice was called “ošišana latinica,” or trimmed latin text.

šišanje
Haircutting/trimming
sisanje
Sucking

Sometimes swapping letters was close enough, but other times language could be misinterpreted.

The Serbian Latin text became quicker and more widespread than the Cyrillic script because of these challenges. There were also very few custom Serbian typefaces, so people began crafting their own solutions to pre-existing fonts.

.yu to .СРБ

A storefront in Belgrade with the co.yu domain suffix painted over. (2015)

The history of the .yu domain parallels the dissolvement of Yugoslavia.

1987
The .yu domain was registered by Slovenian computer scientist, Borka Jerman Blažič. At the time, Yugoslavia was still a unified country, but ethnic tensions are rising.
1991
Slovenia was the main hub for connection, but to establish an international connection they needed permission from Belgrade in Serbia. In April 1991, Jerman Blažič goes to Belgrade to request permission and receives access, but on June 25 the same year, Slovenia and Croatia declare independence.
1994 – 1995
.yu domain hosted in Slovenia, which is no longer part of Yugoslavia. Mirjana Tasić, a Serbian computer scientist, requests access to .yu domain and receives it. Serbia formally enters internet when sanctions are lifted in 1995.
1998 – 2004
Within Yugoslavia, a student group, Otpor, started uprisings against Slobodan Milošević, whom they held responsible for the increased nationalism and murders of ethnic groups in Serbian territories. The Otpor logo was proudly printed in the Serbian Cyrillic alphabet.
2003 – 2006
The Federal Republic of Yugoslavia dissolves into the State Union of Serbia and Montenegro, and then in 2006, two separate countries: Serbia and Montenegro. This results in two additional domain names: .rs and .me
2010
The .yu domain expires.
2012
The .СРБ domain is introduced. Serbia is the second country to have a Cyrillic web domain.

Throughout these structural changes, the Cyrillic Alphabet continued to be used in Serbia but not in other regions of Yugoslavia.

Aleksandra Domanovic’s 2013 movie, From .yu to .me, elaborates on the transition of domain names.

YuFonts

Even with YUSCII type, there weren’t very many typefaces that supported Serbian characters in either the Latin or Cyrillic script. To remedy this, early computer users customized existing websafe typefaces and shared them with one another. Many of them were renamed with the prefix “Yu.” You could only use these typefaces if you had them stored locally.

Most of these typefaces were edits of Swiss inspired typefaces used predominantly in the West, and many were not drawn from scratch. While some were permissable, many were not drawn well by a typographer’s standards.

Starting in 1997, you could use the Cyrillic alphabet of websafe fonts. Verdana was Stojadinović’s preferred font because it had oblique letters.

“It was not very nice, but it was all right.” she said.

In 2009 online custom typography became supported across browsers with the introduction of CSS 3.

Ćirilice na Poklon

In 1995, Stojadinović was commissioned to create an official set of ”Srpski Tajms,“ or Serbian Times, as there were many edits circulating the digital world.

“I did it but I feel bad about it now,” Stojadinović said in a Zoom meeting this April. “It is better to have an original author’s creation.”

Stojadinović has been making typefaces since the start of the digital revolution, and alongisde the City of Belgrade and the Ministry of Culture of Serbia, distributes open source typefaces optimized for the Serbian language. This project is called Ćirilice na Poklon, or Cyrillic as Gift. She also partnered with The Registar of National Serbian Internet Domains (RNIDS) to create a custom typeface in celebration with the newly formed cyrillic domain.

This website is typeset in Dijakritika and Resavska, two of Stojadinović’s recent typefaces. Both typefaces are freely distributed from Stojadinović’s website, Tipometar.org.
